Semi-structured Interview

Creating interview questions might seem like a big deal, but the way you format your questions can greatly influence the responses you get back from your participants


Let us pretend we are studying the experience of college students in their senior year. What might our questions look like? Let us first compare BAD questions with BETTER questions

Bad question:

"A lot of people think senior year is the hardest year of college. How do you feel about it?"

Explanation:

In research this is considered a leading question. By telling the participant how others feel about senior year we have already framed the question for the particant (meaning we might have influenced the way they respond). If we wanted the participants unbiased opinion, it would be better to ask the question in a way which would allow the participant to frame the question themselves

Better question:

"How would you compare your senior year to your previous years in college?

Explanation:

By wording the question this way, we have not talked about senior year being better or worse than previous years, allowing the participant more freedom to explore the question

NOTE:

It is okay to ask about positive and negative experiences if that is what we want to know. For instance, we might ask a participant what they are enjoying about a senior. It is important to note that this question will then NOT represent how the participant generally feels about their senior year, but is framed in the context of what they are enjoying

We might ask what they are enjoying about their senior year after asking them how they generally are feeling about senior year. This way, they will have already framed the question what it is like being a senior themselves

(Potentially) bad question:

"Do you like your classes?"

Explanation:

While we sometimes might want to format questions like this, we generally try and stay away from "yes" or "no" questions. This is because we can ask the question in a way which might yield a richer response

Better question:

"Can you tell me about the environment of your classes? What is a typical class day look like for you?

Explanation:

Asking the question like this allows the participant a larger range of responses. We are more likely to receive a much more robust answer than if we worded the question as we did in the above example
